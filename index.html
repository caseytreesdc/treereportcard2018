Welcome

Casey Trees is a Washington, D.C.-based nonprofit committed to restoring, enhancing and protecting the tree canopy of our nation’s capital. We pursue our mission through community action, education, and research.

Casey Trees’ Tree Report Card measures the quantity and condition of D.C.’s trees and the collective efforts of all groups and individuals working to achieve the District’s 40 percent tree canopy goal. It is based on data collected from both public and private sources, including the District and Federal Governments, universities, developers, nonprofits and others.

Summary

Executive Summary

This reporting year has seen continued progress in DC’s 20-year effort to improve the abundance and health of its trees. With an overall grade of A-, we continue to a see strong showing in tree planting, protection and care.

Despite the progress, a growing threat, noted in prior report cards, persists: an increasingly large portion of land in DC is covered by impervious surfaces such as concrete and asphalt, compromising the city’s streams and rivers. More of our city is covered with concrete than trees – a trend that will only continue to increase unless we do something now.

Given that challenge, this years’ report expands our reach to shed light on how we’re all interconnected, which is why the theme for our current 2019 report is Roots to Rivers. A healthy, robust tree canopy captures and filters rainwater before it enters the drainage system, reduces pollution overflows into the Potomac and Anacostia Rivers, and increases D.C.’s resiliency to climate change. With impervious surfaces that continue to increase – trees play a pivotal role in mitigation of climate change and the overall health and resiliency of our city.

Roots connect all parts of our environment and, because of this, it is important that we protect not just our trees, but also all parts of our connected ecosystem, such as our soil and our waterways.

However, with development in the city increasing at a swift pace, the amount of available land that can be used to support our trees and waterways is rapidly declining. And the more the population increases, the more difficult this challenge will become.

More than 41 percent of the District’s land is covered by impervious surfaces (like buildings and pavement), and, as this number continues to increase, we must do everything in our power to continue to protect or tree canopy. Without it, our roots and trees, rivers, wildlife, and city as a whole, are at risk.

Casey Trees is proud of the support it provides to the City of Trees, but the District needs more - it needs your help! Casey Trees would not be able to accomplish all that it has without your participation and the help of our partners. Through classes, activities, tree care, and advocacy you can help drive our mission to restore, enhance, and protect the tree canopy of the nation’s capital.

Spotlight: Save Our Soil

Spotlight: Save Our Soil

To stem the loss of green space, Casey Trees is introducing the new Save our Soil program. This innovated holistic approach is revolutionizing the way we think about future greenspaces in the District. Through conservation easements we will be able to ensure green space access now and for generations to come. Once protected by an easement, the land remains undeveloped to support mature trees and other natural amenities, while also providing a green respite for the neighborhoods within which they are located.

“One of the best things we can do to preserve tree canopy is to preserve soil.” - Mark Buscaino, Executive Director

Vacant to Vibrant

The Department of Housing and Community Development’s Vacant to Vibrant program aims to utilize vacant lots throughout the District. Over the past year, lands that the city has not been able to develop due to their size, location or other reasons, are being repurposed as perpetual green spaces through Casey Trees Save our Soil Program.

One of the first lots preserved through this program is located in the Deanwood neighborhood on Sheriff Road NE. We saved over 8,000 square feet of green space with magnolia, dogwood, redbud, river birch, and weeping willow trees. Reintroducing a lush green oasis for all community members to enjoy. In time, this space can accommodate even more trees to help us inch closer to our 40% tree canopy goal.

Challenges of Development

With cranes and construction a common sight in the District, it’s no secret that D.C. is developing at a rapid pace. New buildings are a visual marker of DC’s growing population, expected to reach 1 million people by 2045. Because of this, Mayor Bowser has already made a goal to build 36,000 residential housing units by 2025. While more housing is needed to accommodate new and current residents, it also means that impervious surfaces (like buildings and pavement) are growing beginning to replace our city’s green spaces. This creates massive challenges for managing stormwater and the urban heat island, areas that are up to 22 degrees warmer because of impervious surfaces. It also decreases the plantable space we count on to grow tree canopy, and without a growing tree canopy, D.C. loses the vibrancy that makes it such an attractive place to live and work. We must act now to preserve healthy soil, so we can plan for future residents, and for future trees.

Washington, D.C. is our home, and our trees provide more than an environmental value, they inspire a sense of place and belonging within our community. From our parks to our shorelines, trees are connection that filters our water, cleans our air, protects our ecosystems, and makes us resilient to the effects of climate change.

Metrics Explained

   

The Tree Report Card rates Washington D.C.’s urban forest based on four metrics: Tree Coverage, Tree Health, Tree Planting and Tree Protection. Each metric is assigned a numeric score and grade. An overall grade is then determined by averaging the individual metrics.

Tree Coverage

The District’s current canopy coverage is 38 percent resulting in an A grade. This reflects a 2 percent increase from 2015.

Tree Health

Every five years, Casey Trees collects data from 201 sample sites across the District. Results from the 2015 survey did not show any significant difference from the 2010, where 83% of D.C.’s 2.4 million trees were shown to be in good to excellent condition, resulting in a B- grade.

Tree Planting

In the past decade, tree planting on both public and private lands has significantly increased through expanded partnerships.

 

For the City to achieve its 40 percent canopy goal by 2032, at least 10,648 trees must be planted each year for the next 14 years. In 2018, 15,529 trees were collectively planted throughout D.C. This vastly surpasses the target and is the highest planting number to date - resulting in an A+ grade.

Tree Protection

Tree Protection metric is an assessment of the effectiveness of the city’s two landmark Tree Protection Laws: the Urban Forest Preservation Act, and its successor – the Tree Canopy Protection Amendment Act of 2016.

The most significant modification in the 2016 Act was the protection of “Heritage Trees,” those 100 inches in circumference and greater. Over the past year we’re beginning to see the fruits of that legislation, reflected in the caption on this page – the moving of a large oak tree to accommodate a new development. In the past this tree would have been reduced to logs and mulch, but now it will continue mature and thrive through the success of this legislative advancement.

In judging the success of the city’s efforts to protect trees and replace those that are lost, we look at three sub-metrics identified and described below:

Are the fees and fines levied keeping pace with inflation?
Using the Consumer Price Index, we can see that the present-day value of the fees and fines required in the TCPAA have not significantly changed since the law’s passing in May 2016. Therefore, we can award this sub-metric an A grade.

Are the replacement trees surviving?
Although there are active efforts to replace removed Special Trees, it takes a prolonged maintenance effort to ensure these trees reach full maturity. Trees take time to mature and accrue ecosystem benefits. At twenty years old, trees are providing tehse services. Through our comprehensive longitudinal survival study, 77 percent of the trees planted make it to year twenty. Therefore, we can award this sub-metric a C+ grade.

Are fees and fines being used to plant replacement trees?
Per both the Urban Forest Preservation Act of 2002 and the TCPAA, money from the Tree Fund is required to be used to plant trees, therefore, we can assume that the fees and fines are being used to plant replacement trees and we can award this sub-metric an A+ grade.

Recommendations

While we celebrate and continue to see significant progress, much more remains to be done. It’s a competition - plantable lands still exist, but impervious surfaces are growing at an alarming rate. Therefore, we encourage Mayor Bowser to:

1

Adopt a city-wide impervious surface maximum percentage to protect open spaces from being developed and decrease the urban heat island.

3

Convene an expert panel to create incremental tree canopy goals for all D.C. public trees in order to help reach the 40% by 2032 goal.

2

Preserve at least one District-managed public park in all eight Wards in a conservation easement through Casey Trees’ Save our Soil program.

4

Develop an Urban Forestry Master Plan for the District that would include a process for public engagement to allow for input in the Urban Forestry Master Plan and create public agency in the city’s trees.

Appendix

 

Tree Coverage is a measure of a tree’s crown when viewed from above and is the way we track the progress that the District makes towards its 40 percent tree canopy by 2032 goal. The tree crown or canopy produces most of the trees overall benefits.

Tree Health encompasses trees condition, species, size and type. This metric helps us understand how resilient the District’s trees are in terms of potential threats due to pests and diseases. It can also predict the longevity and future composition of D.C.’s trees.

Tree Planting is a count of annual tree planting numbers compared to the total number of trees that must be planted per year to achieve the city’s 40% canopy goal by 2032.

Tree Protection refers to the preservation of trees on a site undergoing construction. This metric considers removing, relocating and replacing trees and their impact on the 40% goal.
